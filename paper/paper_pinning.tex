\begin{abstract}
The options pinning hypothesis proposes that concentrated open interest at specific strike prices suppresses underlying price movement near expiration, as delta-hedging activity by market makers creates a gravitational effect. We test this hypothesis using 1.6~million near-expiry SPY options records matched with E-mini S\&P~500 futures at one-minute resolution across 2{,}294 trading days (2016--2025). We examine five variations: close-versus-open distance to maximum open interest strikes, the relationship between open interest levels and pinning strength, daily range compression, intraday time spent near high open interest strikes, and final-hour convergence. None of the five tests produce evidence consistent with pinning. The single statistically significant result (p~$<$~0.001) shows the \textit{opposite} effect: days with the highest ATM open interest exhibit 16\% \textit{wider} daily ranges than days with the lowest, consistent with gamma-driven volatility amplification rather than suppression. The structural analysis was guided by Database Whisper, a discriminator ladder algorithm that identified open interest as the second most important structural discriminator in options data---more informative than implied volatility---prompting this investigation.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Options pinning---the idea that underlying prices are ``pinned'' to strikes with high open interest near expiration---is one of the most persistent informal beliefs in derivatives markets. The proposed mechanism is straightforward: as expiration approaches, market makers holding short options positions delta-hedge more aggressively, and the hedging flows at high open interest strikes create buying pressure below the strike and selling pressure above, suppressing the underlying's ability to move away.

The hypothesis has been discussed in practitioner literature since at least the early 2000s~\cite{ni2005} and is widely referenced in options trading communities. However, empirical evidence remains mixed. Ni, Pearson, and Poteshman~(2005) found some evidence of pinning in equity options, while subsequent work has produced conflicting results depending on the market, time period, and methodology.

This paper was motivated by an unexpected finding from a structural analysis tool. We applied Database Whisper~\cite{elms2026dw}, a discriminator ladder algorithm that automatically discovers which fields best distinguish records in a structured dataset, to a database of 24.7~million SPY options records. The algorithm identified open interest as the second most important structural discriminator---ranking above implied volatility---for distinguishing options within the same date, type, and expiration bucket. This structural prominence of open interest, combined with the persistent belief in pinning among practitioners, motivated a systematic empirical test.

\section{Data}

\subsection{Options Data}

We use 24.7~million SPY options records spanning January~2008 to December~2025, sourced from historical options chain data. Each record contains: contract identifier, strike, type (call/put), last price, mark, bid, ask, volume, open interest, implied volatility, and full Greeks (delta, gamma, theta, vega, rho). For the pinning analysis, we focus on near-expiry options ($\text{DTE} \leq 3$), yielding 1{,}566{,}296 records.

\subsection{Underlying Data}

We use E-mini S\&P~500 futures (@ES) at one-minute resolution from October~2016 to April~2026 (3.3~million bars). We restrict to regular trading hours (9:30--16:00~ET), producing 937{,}182 bars across 2{,}436 trading days. SPY and ES track closely; we convert ES prices to SPY-equivalent by dividing by~10 for strike-level comparisons.

\subsection{Matching}

We match each trading day's SPY options open interest profile with the corresponding day's ES intraday price action. The merged dataset covers 2{,}294 trading days.

\section{Structural Motivation: Database Whisper}

Before designing the pinning tests, we profiled the options dataset using Database Whisper~\cite{elms2026dw,elms2026five}, a greedy pair-reduction algorithm that discovers which fields most effectively disambiguate records that share the same identity key.

When options are grouped by date, type, and days-to-expiration bucket, the algorithm discovers the following discriminator ladder:

\begin{center}
\texttt{delta\_bucket} (72\%) $\to$ \texttt{oi\_bucket} (59\%) $\to$ \texttt{iv\_bucket} (52\%) $\to$ \texttt{vol\_bucket} (43\%)
\end{center}

The percentages indicate ambiguity reduction rate at each rung. The key finding: \textbf{open interest is structurally more informative than implied volatility} for distinguishing options within the same group. Every options textbook and most quantitative models prioritize implied volatility over open interest. The data's disambiguation structure disagrees.

This does not imply that open interest predicts returns better than IV. Disambiguation importance and predictive importance are different properties~\cite{elms2026five}. However, the structural prominence of open interest suggested that its market microstructure effects---including the hypothesized pinning---deserved a closer look.

\section{Five Tests of Pinning}

We test five variations of the pinning hypothesis. For each trading day, we identify the SPY strike with the highest near-expiry ($\text{DTE} \leq 3$) total open interest. All tests ask: does price behavior relate to this maximum-OI strike?

\subsection{Test 1: Close-vs-Open Distance}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} If pinning occurs, the closing price should be closer to the maximum-OI strike than the opening price---the market gravitates toward the pin.

\textbf{Result:} Price moved \textit{toward} the maximum-OI strike on 44.9\% of days and \textit{away} on 55.1\%. Average distance change: $-\$0.06$ (negative = moved away). Not statistically significant ($t = -0.84$, $p = 0.40$).

\subsection{Test 2: OI-Level Effect}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} If pinning is driven by hedging flows, the effect should strengthen when open interest is higher---more contracts to hedge, stronger gravitational pull.

\textbf{Result:} High-OI days (above median total OI) moved toward the max-OI strike 47.5\% of the time vs.\ 42.2\% for low-OI days. Directionally consistent with pinning but not statistically significant ($t = 1.32$, $p = 0.19$).

\subsection{Test 3: Daily Range Compression}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Pinning should compress the daily range---if price is being pulled toward a strike, it should wander less far from it.

\textbf{Result:} The \textit{opposite} occurs. Daily range increases monotonically with open interest:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
OI Tercile & Mean Range & Median Range & Days \\
\midrule
Low & 0.889\% & 0.688\% & 765 \\
Medium & 0.999\% & 0.791\% & 764 \\
High & 1.033\% & 0.847\% & 765 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Daily range by open interest level. High OI is associated with wider ranges.}
\label{tab:range}
\end{table}

High-OI days have 16.2\% wider ranges than low-OI days. This difference is statistically significant ($t = -3.67$, $p = 0.0003$). The result is inconsistent with pinning and consistent with gamma-driven amplification: market makers' hedging activity creates feedback loops that widen rather than compress the trading range.

\subsection{Test 4: Intraday Time Near Max-OI Strike}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} If pinning operates intraday, price should spend more time near the maximum-OI strike on high-OI days.

\textbf{Result:} On a sample of 200 days, neither high-OI nor low-OI days showed meaningful time concentration near the max-OI strike. No significant difference detected.

\subsection{Test 5: Final-Hour Convergence}

\textbf{Hypothesis:} Pinning should be strongest near the close as expiration approaches and gamma peaks. Price at close should be nearer to the max-OI strike than at midday or 3:00~PM.

\textbf{Result:} Price converged toward the max-OI strike from noon to close on 43.0\% of high-OI days and 45.0\% of low-OI days. No significant difference ($t = -0.28$, $p = 0.78$).

\subsection{Summary of Findings}

None of the five tests support the pinning hypothesis. The only statistically significant result (Test~3, $p < 0.001$) shows the opposite effect.

\subsection{Alternative Interpretation: Gamma Amplification}

The V3 result---wider ranges on high-OI days---is consistent with gamma-driven amplification. When market makers are short a large number of near-expiry options, they must delta-hedge aggressively as the underlying moves. This creates a positive feedback loop: upward moves force buying, which pushes price higher, which forces more buying. Downward moves force selling. The result is amplified rather than suppressed movement.

This mechanism is the opposite of pinning but arises from the same market microstructure. Both pinning and amplification are driven by delta-hedging of concentrated open interest. The empirical data suggests that amplification dominates.

\subsection{Confounds}

We note an important confound: high open interest near expiry is not exogenous. Traders accumulate positions in anticipation of events (FOMC, CPI, earnings), and those events independently cause large moves. The wider ranges on high-OI days may reflect this selection effect rather than a causal amplification mechanism.

Disentangling event-driven OI accumulation from the structural effect of OI on price dynamics would require controlling for scheduled economic events. We leave this to future work.

\subsection{Role of Structural Profiling}

This investigation was prompted by a structural profiling tool (Database Whisper) identifying open interest as more structurally informative than implied volatility in options data. This illustrates a use case for automated structural analysis: the algorithm surfaced a feature ranking that contradicted conventional wisdom, which in turn motivated an empirical investigation that produced a statistically significant result in the opposite direction of the prevailing hypothesis.

The structural profiler did not predict the anti-pinning result. It identified open interest as structurally prominent, which prompted the question. The data answered it.

\section{Conclusion}

We find no evidence that options open interest pins the underlying across five tests on 2{,}294 trading days using 1.6~million near-expiry options records and minute-resolution futures data. The single statistically significant finding shows the opposite: high open interest days have significantly wider daily ranges ($p < 0.001$), consistent with gamma-driven amplification rather than suppression.

These results do not rule out pinning at shorter timescales, in individual equities, or in specific market regimes. They do suggest that the general form of the pinning hypothesis---that concentrated open interest suppresses movement of the S\&P~500 near expiration---is not supported by the data.
